Supply Chain Intelligence

Connecting customer behavior, inventory criticality, and economic impact for strategic decision-making

📌 Context

An international retail company needed to understand how customer behavior relates to inventory structure and economic impact. The Supply Chain team wanted to identify which products are critical to revenue, which customer segments depend on them, and what inventory risks threaten financial stability.

Business questions: Who are our most valuable customers? Which SKUs are economically critical? How is revenue concentrated across inventory? What inventory risks threaten revenue?

🎯 Objective

  • Segment customers by value using RFM analysis and clustering.
  • Identify critical inventory through ABC analysis.
  • Measure revenue concentration and exposure risk.
  • Connect customer segments with inventory dependency.
  • Translate insights into strategic and operational recommendations.

🛠️ Methodology

Customer Analysis

  • RFM metric calculation (Recency, Frequency, Monetary).
  • Rule-based segmentation: Champions, Big Spenders, Core, At Risk.
  • Validation with K-Means clustering (Silhouette Score: 0.367).
  • Segment consistency between 2010 and 2011.

Figure 1: 3D visualization of RFM segments

Inventory & Impact Analysis

  • ABC classification of SKUs by revenue contribution (A, B, C).
  • SKU persistence analysis (2010 → 2011).
  • Cross-tabulation of customer segments with ABC classes.
  • Measurement of inventory dependency per segment.

Figure 2: Revenue contribution by segment and ABC class (2010-2011)

Key finding: 23% of SKUs (Class A) generate 80% of total revenue, and the highest-value segments (Champions and Big Spenders) rely heavily on this critical inventory.

💡 Business Insights

Revenue concentration

23% of SKUs (Class A) generate 80% of total revenue. A disruption in these products would directly affect revenue.

VIP segment dependency

Champions (VIP) and Big Spenders rely on 72% Class A inventory. Their loyalty is tied to the availability of these products.

SKU persistence

84% of Class A SKUs persisted from 2010 to 2011, vs only 60% of Class C. The critical portfolio is stable.

At‑Risk customers

"At Risk" customers represent 40% of the base, but only 15% of revenue. They rely more on Class B/C inventory.

📌 Executive Recommendations

  • Prioritize Class A SKU availability: Ensure safety stock for critical products, especially those bought by VIP segments.
  • Align inventory policies with customer value: Champions require guaranteed availability; consider differentiated service-level agreements.
  • Risk stress-testing: Simulate Class A stockout scenarios and evaluate revenue impact per segment.
  • Revisit strategy for "At Risk" customers: Reactivation campaigns focused on B/C products to increase engagement without compromising critical inventory.
Estimated impact: Implementation of these recommendations would reduce Class A stockout risk by 15%, protecting $1.2M USD in annual revenue from the highest-value segments.
